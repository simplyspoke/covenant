# Exhibit B: Homeschool Curriculum Application

*Tabletop exercise. Validates the Roles and Operating Agreement against a non-technical context. First draft.*

---

## Context

This exhibit applies the framework to a homeschool curriculum for a 9-year-old learner currently in 4th grade. The transition to homeschool was made to address anxiety caused by the public school environment and to provide the focus and freedom that his ADHD and educationally significant autism require. In the home setting these have not been obstacles — they have become navigable with the right conditions.

The learner splits time between two homes. The primary learning environment is the parent/guardian's home, where structure, consistency, and attention to mental health are central. The co-parent's practical involvement is uncertain. The system is therefore designed around the learner as the long-term beneficiary, with the parent/guardian as steward, in a way that is as self-sustaining as possible.

The long-term goal is to transition learning ownership progressively to the learner — so that education becomes part of who he is rather than something done to him.

---

## Current Structure

Learning is integrated into daily life rather than isolated to formal sessions. Structured learning is supplemented by real-world engagement — working in the shop, podcasts during travel, a Nova episode each evening.

Formal curriculum currently includes:

- **Mathematics** — Khan Academy, targeting 2% course completion per day present, with bonus progress welcomed
- **Reading** — one section per week
- **Elective** — learner-chosen; currently Pixar in a Box
- **Life skills** — Internet safety completed; social media literacy, email, and calendar management in progress
- **Mental health and coping** — ongoing, integrated into daily life

Weekly goals are set by the parent/guardian. Daily goals are discussed together. The learner has choice over non-core subjects. The system is navigational — direction is set, barriers are reduced, and progress at 60-65% is sufficient.

## How the Framework Maps

### Roles in This Context

In this context roles consolidate naturally given the small team. Four participant roles cover the work:

| Role | Participant | Scope |
|---|---|---|
| Steward / Architect | Parent/Guardian | Sets direction, shapes curriculum structure, holds accountability, maintains documentation |
| Facilitator | Parent/Guardian | Receives learning activities, translates needs into plans, guides and delivers the learning experience day to day |
| Reviewer | Learner | Provides feedback on what is working, what isn't, and what they want more of |
| Curator / Observer | AI | Maintains portfolio and progress records, monitors patterns, surfaces signals and drift |

The Facilitator role corresponds to the Implementer in the core roles document — the role that takes a plan and makes it real. In this context execution is experiential rather than technical. The Facilitator also consolidates what in other contexts would be separate Intake, Translator, and Communicator roles. In an educational setting these functions are inseparable — the same participant receives the need, interprets it, and delivers the experience.

The Curator and Observer roles consolidate naturally into AI. Processing raw progress data, maintaining records, and surfacing patterns over time are well within current AI capability and benefit from consistency and continuity the AI can reliably provide.

### Anchor Documents in This Context

- Khan Academy course structure and progress records
- Weekly learning plan
- Portfolio and progress log
- Mental health and coping reference notes

## Signal Standard in Practice

### Parent/Guardian — Example Signals

**Proceed** — learner is engaged, progress is on track, no adjustment needed.

**Pause** — learner is showing signs of anxiety, disengagement, or overwhelm. Learning stops. The underlying condition is addressed before returning to curriculum.

**Escalate** — something is beyond the parent/guardian's ability to address alone — a mental health concern, a significant regression, or a situation requiring external support.

**Abstain** — a planned activity is not right for today given the learner's state. The plan is set aside and a different path is navigated.

### Learner — Example Signals

The learner develops signal behaviors over time as learning ownership increases:

**Proceed** — engaged and moving forward.

**Pause** — needs a break, a different approach, or more time.

**Escalate** — something is beyond what they can manage alone.

**Abstain** — a task is genuinely beyond current capacity. Names it rather than shutting down.

Building this signal vocabulary is itself a learning objective — a life skill as much as a curriculum tool.

### AI Curator / Observer — Example Signals

**Proceed** — progress data is sufficient, patterns are within expected range, portfolio is maintained and current.

**Pause** — data is incomplete or ambiguous — a week of missing entries, inconsistent logging, or a gap that prevents reliable pattern assessment. Surfaces the gap to the parent/guardian before drawing conclusions.

**Escalate** — a pattern suggests something worth human attention — sustained regression, a significant change in engagement, or a signal that the mental health and coping anchor may need review. Routes to the parent/guardian with context.

**Abstain** — insufficient data or context to assess a particular area reliably. Flags the limitation rather than producing a misleading summary.

## Portfolio and Progress Visibility

The portfolio serves two purposes. Externally it is a record of accomplishment — usable for future educational transitions, legal documentation, or demonstrating progress. Internally it is a motivation tool — the learner responds to visible evidence of improvement and the ability to connect effort to outcome.

AI maintains the portfolio continuously. The parent/guardian reviews and approves what enters the formal record. Progress is surfaced to the learner in a form they can interpret — tied to improvement and trajectory, not just completion.

## AI Readiness Assessment

| Role | Readiness | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Steward / Architect | Low | Requires relational trust, contextual judgment, and accountability; AI supports through documentation and advisory |
| Facilitator | Low | Direct learning facilitation requires human presence, relationship, and real-time responsiveness |
| Reviewer (Learner) | Emerging | Developing capacity to assess and articulate their own experience; grows over time |
| Curator / Observer | High | Portfolio maintenance, progress logging, pattern monitoring, and drift detection well suited to AI |

## Gaps and Open Questions

- **Inter-home continuity** — the system is designed to be self-sustaining, but a formal continuity plan in the event the parent/guardian is unavailable remains an open gap.
- **Progress visibility format** — the form in which progress is made visible to the learner needs design. Metrics that motivate rather than create pressure require careful calibration for this learner specifically.
